Models Used For Training

Linear Regression

Imagine you have a bunch of dots on a graph, each representing a pair of numbers, like the price of a house and its size. Linear regression is like drawing a straight line through those dots, trying to capture the general trend. This line helps us predict one number (like the price) based on another (like the size). It's a simple but powerful tool used in everything from predicting sales to understanding how variables are related in science.

Ridge Regression

Let's think of ridge regression as a helpful twist on linear regression. You know how sometimes, when you're drawing a line through points on a graph, it might not perfectly fit all the dots? Ridge regression steps in to fix that. It adds a special ingredient to the mix, kind of like putting a little cushion under the line, helping it hug the dots better. This 'cushion' prevents the line from getting too wobbly, giving us more reliable predictions, especially when dealing with lots of features or variables. It's like having a steadier hand when drawing on a bumpy road – making our predictions smoother and more accurate.

Lasso Regression

Picture you're trying to find the best fit line through a scatterplot, but you know some of those dots might not be as important as others. Lasso regression is like a smart line-drawing tool that not only finds the best fit but also ignores less important dots. It does this by gently nudging some of the dots to zero, effectively removing them from the equation. This way, you get a simpler, more focused line that still captures the essence of the relationship between your variables. It's like tidying up your data to find the most important patterns without getting distracted by noise.

Support Vector Regression

Imagine you're a teacher trying to draw a line between two groups of students in the playground. The goal is to make sure that line is as far away from both groups as possible, giving a clear separation. Support Vector Regression works a bit like that but in math. It finds the best line to separate data points into two groups. But here's the twist: instead of just drawing a line, it uses special points (called support vectors) to create a boundary that maximizes the space between the groups. This clever technique helps us make predictions in situations where simple lines just won't cut it, making it a handy tool in predicting things like house prices or stock market trends.

K Nearest Neighbor Regression

Picture yourself in a neighborhood where you want to predict the price of a house based on its size. KNN (K-Nearest Neighbors) regression is like asking your neighbors for advice. You look at the sizes and prices of nearby houses (your 'neighbors') and average their prices to estimate the price of the house you're interested in. It's like saying, 'If houses around here are usually this price for that size, then this one might be too.' KNN regression is a friendly, intuitive way to make predictions based on what's close by.

Decision Tree Regression

Think of a decision tree as a game of "20 Questions." You start with a big question like, "Is the weather sunny?" If yes, you ask another question, maybe, "Is it hot?" If no, you ask another, like, "Is it windy?" Decision tree regression works similarly, but instead of guessing things, it asks questions about data to predict outcomes. It breaks down complex decisions into a series of simpler ones, helping us make predictions about things like house prices or stock market movements. It's like having a smart guide that navigates through possibilities step by step to reach the best guess.

Random Forest Regression

Picture a forest filled with different kinds of trees. Now, imagine each tree in this forest is like a small decision-maker. Individually, they might not be super accurate, but when you gather all their opinions together, you get a pretty good answer. That's what a Random Forest Regression model does with data. It combines the predictions of many 'decision trees' to make a more accurate guess about something, like predicting house prices based on factors like location, size, and number of rooms.

Gradient Boost Regression

Picture a team of players working together to win a game. Gradient Boosting Regression is like assembling a team of specialized players, each getting better and better at the game. This model learns from its mistakes, adjusting itself with each round to improve its performance. By combining the strengths of individual 'players'—in this case, weak prediction models—into a strong team, it's able to predict outcomes with remarkable accuracy. Just like a winning team, Gradient Boosting Regression excels in predicting everything from housing prices to customer preferences.

Ada Boost Regression

Picture a group of friends trying to solve a problem together. Each friend has their strengths and weaknesses. AdaBoost Regression is like having each friend specialize in a specific aspect of the problem. Then, they all work together, learning from each other's successes and failures, to come up with the best solution. This technique helps us make more accurate predictions by combining the strengths of multiple 'friends,' or weak learners, into one strong predictor.

XGBoost Regression

Meet XGBoost, the superhero of prediction models! Imagine you're in a talent show trying to guess the next number in a sequence. XGBoost is like having a superpower that helps you make the most accurate guesses. It's a smart algorithm that learns from its mistakes, continuously improving its predictions with each round. Whether you're forecasting sales or predicting customer behavior, XGBoost is your trusty sidekick, always striving to make your predictions sharper and more reliable.
